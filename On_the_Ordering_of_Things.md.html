On the Ordering of Things - The Tao of Mirza

Chapter I — On Naming and Clarity

Those who hurry to build
before naming what is to be built
labor twice.

The first task of the engineer
is not the shaping of symbols,
but the clarification of intent.
Common speech is wide and careless;
formal language is narrow and exact.
To pass from one to the other
is the highest work of the craft.

Once the matter is made precise,
its expression is routine.
Confusion lies not in execution,
but in unclear intent.

Chapter II — On Models and Abstraction

All systems arise from the affairs of people.
Therefore one must first observe the world as it is lived:
actions, constraints, time, and failure.

From these, models are formed.
At the beginning they are close to reality;
later they are refined.
What is essential is preserved.
What is incidental is removed.

To cling too tightly to reality
is to carry unnecessary weight.
To abandon it too early
is to lose the path.

This is the proper progression.

Chapter III — On Parts and Proof

A system is not made whole at once.
It is assembled from parts.

Yet a part proven only in one place
is not truly a part.
Only when placed in another setting
does its nature become known.

Testing is not merely to confirm correctness,
but to expose hidden dependence.
That which cannot be recombined
is already broken.

Chapter IV — On Problems and Discovery

The problem and the solution
are not separate matters.

A well-stated problem
constrains the solution without force.
A poorly stated one
invites endless correction.

Understanding does not appear fully formed.
Through iteration, false assumptions are removed
and the true shape of the work emerges.

Thus engineering is both
discovery and construction.

Chapter V — On Judgment and Tools

Though machines now assist the hand,
they do not choose what should be built.

Automation accelerates both wisdom and error.
Speed magnifies judgment;
it does not replace it.

Tools extend reach,
but they do not provide direction.
Judgment remains the human burden.

Chapter VI — On Mastery and Foundation

Therefore the foundations must be mastered.

They allow little freedom,
yet without them no freedom exists.
When technique becomes instinct,
attention is freed.

Only then may higher patterns
be recognized.

Those who neglect the basics
construct fragile systems.
Those who practice them daily
create space for insight.

Chapter VII — On Order and Endurance

When language is precise,
models hold.

When models hold,
systems endure change.

When judgment governs tools,
speed serves wisdom.

Thus order is achieved
without strain,
and complexity is held
without collapse.
